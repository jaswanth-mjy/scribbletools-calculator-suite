📚 Simple vs. Compound Interest

Simple interest is calculated only on the original principal. It grows linearly over time. For a $10,000 deposit at 6% for 10 years, simple interest yields $6,000 (total $16,000).

Compound interest is calculated on the principal plus accumulated interest, creating exponential growth. The same deposit compounded monthly yields ~$8,167 in interest (total ~$18,167). The more frequently interest compounds, the faster the balance grows.

  • Annually: Interest added once per year.
  • Monthly: Interest added 12 times per year — most common for savings accounts.
  • Daily: Interest added every day — used by many banks.
  • Continuously: Theoretical maximum, using the mathematical constant e.

❓ Interest Calculator FAQ

What is the difference between APR and APY?

+

APR (Annual Percentage Rate) is the stated nominal rate without accounting for compounding. APY (Annual Percentage Yield) reflects the actual return after compounding. For example, a 6% APR compounded monthly yields an APY of 6.17%. APY is always ≥ APR. Banks advertise APY on savings (looks higher) and APR on loans (looks lower).

How much difference does compounding frequency make?

+

On $10,000 at 6% for 10 years: Annual compounding = $17,908, Monthly = $18,194, Daily = $18,221, Continuous = $18,221. The jump from annual to monthly is most significant; after that, returns diminish rapidly. For most practical purposes, monthly and daily produce nearly identical results.

What is the Rule of 72?

+

Divide 72 by the interest rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your money. At 6%: 72 ÷ 6 = 12 years. At 8%: 72 ÷ 8 = 9 years. This quick approximation works well for rates between 2% and 15%.

How do regular contributions affect growth?

+

Regular contributions dramatically accelerate wealth building. $10,000 at 6% for 20 years grows to ~$32,071 alone. Add $200/month and the total becomes ~$124,699 — with $58,000 in contributions and ~$56,628 in interest. The earlier and more consistently you contribute, the greater the compounding benefit.

Is interest earned on savings taxable?

+

Yes. In the US, interest earned on savings accounts, CDs, and bonds is generally taxable as ordinary income. Banks issue Form 1099-INT for interest over $10. Tax-advantaged accounts (IRAs, 401(k)s) can defer or eliminate interest taxation. Municipal bond interest is typically exempt from federal tax.
