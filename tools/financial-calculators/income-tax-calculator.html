📚 How Federal Tax Brackets Work

The US uses a progressive (marginal) tax system. Your income isn’t all taxed at one rate — instead, it’s split into layers (brackets), and each layer is taxed at its own rate:

  1. The first portion of taxable income is taxed at 10%.
  2. The next portion at 12%.
  3. Then 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, and finally 37% on the highest earners.

This means your marginal rate (the rate on your last dollar) is always higher than your effective rate (total tax ÷ total income). For example, a single filer with $100,000 taxable income in 2025 is in the 22% bracket, but their effective rate is only about 16%.

🧪 Tax Calculation Formula

Tax = Σ (Income in Bracketi) × Ratei − Credits
Taxable Income = Gross Income − Pre-Tax Deductions − Standard/Itemized Deduction
Tax = Total federal income tax liability
Ratei = Marginal rate for bracket i (10%, 12%, 22%, 24%, 32%, 35%, 37%)
Credits = Non-refundable + refundable tax credits

❓ Income Tax FAQ

What’s the difference between marginal and effective tax rate?

+

Your marginal rate is the rate on your last dollar of taxable income — the highest bracket you’ve entered. Your effective rate is your total tax divided by total income, which is always lower because earlier dollars are taxed at lower rates. For financial decisions, the marginal rate matters most (e.g., for evaluating a raise or deduction).

Should I take the standard or itemized deduction?

+

Take whichever is larger. After the 2017 Tax Cuts & Jobs Act nearly doubled the standard deduction, about 87% of filers now take the standard deduction. You should consider itemizing if you have large mortgage interest, state/local taxes (up to $10,000 SALT cap), or significant charitable donations that together exceed the standard amount.

Does this calculator include FICA / Social Security / Medicare taxes?

+

The main calculation shows only federal income tax. FICA taxes (7.65% for most workers: 6.2% Social Security + 1.45% Medicare) are shown separately in the FICA note below the results. Self-employed individuals pay the full 15.3%. These are in addition to federal income tax.

What about state income taxes?

+

This calculator covers federal taxes only. State income tax varies widely — 7 states have no income tax (TX, FL, NV, WA, WY, SD, AK), while California’s top rate is 13.3% and New York City adds a city tax. Total state+local rates generally add 0%–13% on top of federal.

What is the SALT cap?

+

The State and Local Tax (SALT) deduction is capped at $10,000 ($5,000 for married filing separately) under the Tax Cuts & Jobs Act (2018–2025). This limits the federal deduction for state income taxes, local taxes, and property taxes combined. The cap is expected to be a topic of debate when the TCJA provisions potentially sunset after 2025.

When do the TCJA provisions expire?

+

Most individual provisions of the 2017 Tax Cuts & Jobs Act are set to sunset after December 31, 2025. Without Congressional action, brackets would revert to pre-2018 levels (with inflation adjustments), the standard deduction would roughly halve, personal exemptions would return, and the SALT cap would be removed. Congress may extend, modify, or let them expire.
